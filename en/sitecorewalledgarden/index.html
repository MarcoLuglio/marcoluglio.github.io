Sitecore walled garden

So, you saw Gartner Digital Experience Platform (DXP) magic quadrant and decided to learn about Content Management Systems. Or maybe you are tired of working with Wordpress and wanted to check out the alternatives, maybe one in C#? Starting with these platforms can be slow and challenging for a number of reasons. Stick around and I will do my best to get you jump started. In this article I’ll talk about Sitecore.

Gartner Magic Quadrant

In Gartner Magic Quadrant, Adobe Experience Manager seems to be the current leader, but it is written in Java (no surprise there, given the choice for previous products such as ColdFusion). Others being Acquia (PHP), Episerver (C#) Liferay (Java) and Sitecore (C#) naturally. Again, the world seems to be divided between the two usual suspects, with an alternative for developers that frown upon more formal languages.

Now, if you are wondering why not just call it the CMS magic quadrant and make everyone’s lifes easier, it used to be called just that. And for what developers may care the most, that is probably where we will spend most of our time. But, as you may already have suspected, developers are not the focus of these platforms. In fact, programming languages are not even mentioned in the homepage of the aforementioned products, and you will only find about them after some digging. Marketing folks and copywriters are the main target of these platforms.

For a good while now, brands have expanded way beyond their web sites and email marketing campaign fronts to all sorts of social media and mobile channels and who knows what else in the future. We needed a name for that. DXP.

Why so hard on new devs?

Beam me up Scotty, welcome aboard the Enterprise. Enterprise applications world that is, where software trials, distribution and source are heavily guarded and as closed and opaque as possible. Because you, humble dev, is not calling the shots here. Commercial agreements to buy these platforms have been made by people way above your pay grade. Those are the people that need to be convinced by the platforms.

Nevertheless, software requires devs right? So what’s left for us? To explore strange new classes. To seek out new APIs. To boldly go where no dev has gone before! Onto badly charted (if charted at all) lands. And while documentation and official training may seem lacking, there’s plenty of people in the same boat whiling to share knowledge.
